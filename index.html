02

Research Statement

Perishable goods, blood units, fresh produce, short shelf life inventory, are managed under uncertainty that classical optimization struggles to capture. Demand is noisy, supply is disrupted, and decisions made today constrain what is possible tomorrow. My research asks how autonomous agents can make good pricing, replenishment, and coordination decisions in these settings, without relying on the simplifying assumptions that make problems tractable but unrealistic.

I work at the intersection of operations research and machine learning, to design decision making systems for inventory control, dynamic pricing, and supply chain coordination. This matters because perishable and life critical supply chains, blood banks, food distribution, retail, absorb real costs from waste, shortage, and slow adaptation to disruption. My goal is to build methods that are both theoretically grounded and usable in practice, so that the systems making these decisions can learn, adapt, and remain resilient as conditions change.
